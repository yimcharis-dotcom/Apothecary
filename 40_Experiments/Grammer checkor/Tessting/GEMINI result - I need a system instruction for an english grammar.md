### Explanation of the Linguistics \& Rules

This system instruction is built on specific linguistic principles to ensure high-quality feedback. Here is the breakdown of the rules embedded in the prompt:

#### 1. Pragmatics and Register

Native speech varies heavily based on **register** (the level of formality). A sentence like *"I require the salt"* is grammatically correct but pragmatically weird at a dinner table.[^1_14]

* **The Rule:** The system is instructed to ask for context because "native" is not a single standard; it changes from the boardroom to the bar.[^1_6]


#### 2. Collocation vs. Grammar

Non-native speakers often struggle with **collocations**‚Äîwords that naturally go together.

* **Example:** A learner might say *"I did a mistake."*
* **Linguistics:** This is grammatically fine (Subject + Verb + Object), but native speakers say *"I **made** a mistake."*
* **The Instruction:** The prompt directs the AI to identify "Unnatural Phrasing" specifically to catch these collocation errors that standard spell-checkers miss.[^1_8][^1_12]


#### 3. Syntactic Reduction (Ellipsis)

Native speakers frequently omit words that can be understood from context, a process called **ellipsis** or reduction.[^1_1]

* **Example:** Instead of *"Do you want to go to the store?"*, a native speaker might say *"Wanna go to the store?"* or just *"Store?"*
* **The Instruction:** The "Native Speaker Variations" section explicitly asks for these naturally reduced forms, which helps the user sound less robotic.

### Example Output

Here is how the AI would respond to a user input based on the instruction above.

**User Input:**
*"I am having much doubt about if to accept the job."*

**AI Response:**

### üîç Analysis

* **Grammar (Quantifiers):** "Much doubt" is grammatically possible but very uncommon in this context. We typically use "much" in negative sentences (e.g., "I don't have much time"). In positive assertions, "a lot of doubts" or simply "doubts" is preferred.
* **Syntax (Indirect Questions):** "About if to accept" is awkward syntax. Native English rarely places "if" directly after a preposition like "about." It is smoother to use "whether to accept" or "about accepting."
* **Naturalness:** "I am having" (continuous aspect) implies a temporary state, which works, but "I have" is often stronger for possession of an abstract feeling like doubt.


### ‚úÖ Corrected Version

"I have a lot of doubts about whether to accept the job."

### üó£Ô∏è Native Speaker Alternatives

* **Casual/Spoken:** "I'm really on the fence about taking the job." (Uses the idiom "on the fence").[^1_4]
* **Professional:** "I am hesitating on whether to accept the position."
* **Direct/Idiomatic:** "I'm having second thoughts about the job offer."
