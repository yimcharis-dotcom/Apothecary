Britain's "Blitz Spirit" was significantly mythologized: while no mass psychiatric crisis occurred, looting was rampant (4,584 cases prosecuted in just four months), crime rose 60% during the war, mass "trekking" from cities was common, and 60% of civilians simply stayed home rather than using shelters—meanwhile Beaverbrook's aircraft production reforms proved decisive, with British fighter output reaching 2.5 times Germany's by mid-1940.
130 sources

Britain's survival in summer 1940 hinged on Churchill defeating Halifax's peace negotiation bid in a critical 3-day War Cabinet crisis (26–28 May), combined with the Dowding System's integrated air defence, Beaverbrook's 2.5x increase in fighter production over German output, and Germany's fundamentally unresolvable logistics problem of crossing the Channel with slow Rhine barges against the world's largest navy — while Hitler himself showed surprisingly little interest in invasion planning and was already pivoting toward Russia by 31 July 1940.
90 sources

The Battle of Britain was the first major German defeat of WWII, and Britain's survival as an undefeated western base forced Hitler into the fatal two-front war—with revisionist historians arguing the RAF was never as close to defeat as popular mythology suggests, since the Luftwaffe was never positioned to gain the upper hand and Fighter Command ended the battle with more aircraft than it started with.
101 sources

The Battle of Britain's outcome hinged on Germany's critical strategic error of switching from airfield attacks to bombing London on 7 September 1940, giving Fighter Command a decisive respite during its most vulnerable period — though the RAF's integrated radar-based Dowding System, which achieved ~90% interception rates and acted as a force multiplier equivalent to tripling fighter strength, meant the Luftwaffe likely could never have achieved more than a Pyrrhic victory regardless.
